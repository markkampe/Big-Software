Bug Severity and Priority

Mark Kampe

In a large project there are likely to be many bugs ... sufficiently many that they will not all be fixed promptly. It becomes necessary to decide which bugs to fix sooner, and which to fix later. Long ago battle-field doctors learned to divide casualties into three groups:

  1. those who would almost surely die (treatment wouldn't make a difference).
  2. those who would almost surely live (treatment can be delayed).
  3. those who could be saved if treated promptly.
Taking a few minutes to perform this sorting (called triage, in reference to the three groups) greatly improved the effectiveness of battle-field medicine. A similar process is followed with bugs ... although hopefully without a class of problems that will doom the product regardless of any action on our part :-)

Which bugs to fix first

Bugs are, to a first approximation, dealt with on the basis of their severity and priority. Because these two words determine which bugs will be fixed, control over the defintion of these terms translates into control over what work does and does not get done; Arguments that might seem to be about definitions may be power struggles in disguise.

There may be no universally accepted definitions for these terms, but the following should give you a foundation from which you can enter into discussions on the subject:

  • severity is probably the better defined of these two terms, and is usually taken to be a measure the consequences to the victim. System failure and loss of important data might be consdered to be very severe, confusing error messages might be considered to be of relatively low severity.

    There are many (per organization) standards for grading severity, but a typical one might be:

    1. permanent data or service loss.
    2. unable to use primary functionality
    3. impared use of primary functionality, or unable to use secondary functionality.
    4. minor inconvenience.
    5. suggestion.

    Many organizations adopt the convention that the severity of a bug is owned by the submitter (the submitter gets to determine how seriously (s)he was impacted).

  • priority is a concept with a much wider range of meanings. The most cogent meaning I have found is that it is a measure of the importance (to the success of the project) of fixing this bug. The severity is surely one of the factors in determining this, but there may be other equally important factors:
    • how likely it is that others will encouter the problem
    • the availability of a work-around
    • the expectations that have been set for the product in question

    In keeping with such a defininition the priority of a bug is owned by the product owner, and a priority scale might be:

    1. must fix immediately (e.g. within 24 hours).
    2. must fix in next release (product cannot ship without this fix).
    3. should fix in next release (too many of these will stop shipment).
    4. fix when it is convenient (e.g. we are working on this code for other reasons).
    5. may get to it some day.

Priority in Agile projects

In a large company with large teams and formal process, the above priority definitions can provide a basis for:

  • development decisions about what to work on next
  • management decisions about whether or not the product is ready to ship
In a more agile project, with regular (e.g. weekly) deliveries, decisions about what work to do next (in the next sprint) are:
  • made, case-by-case, by the product owner
  • not represented by numeric priorities, but rather by ordering of tasks in the backlog (e.g. a Project Board of Github Issues)

There would seem to be a significant philosophical difference between:

  • a bug prioritization team determining what work must be completed before a release can be shipped
  • a product owner deciding, week by week, what the best use of resources (for both bug fixing and new features) for the next sprint would be
But if we look at the factors the product owner will consider when ordering tasks in the backlog, they are probably very similar to those that a prioritization team would use in assigning priorities.
